### Societal-values-inform-healthy-and-accelerated-aging

#### Description


This repository contains the code to implement the reported results in our paper.

#### Abstract
Societal values (i.e., collective wellbeing, social cohesion, acceptance of rights and choices, and institutional confidence) shape how communities’ function and influence individual health. However, it remains unknown whether societal values are related to delayed or accelerated aging, including regional and sex-specific variation. Here, we tested whether societal values are associated with accelerated or delayed aging across European and Latin American countries. Biobehavioral age gaps (BBAGs) measured the difference between chronological age and age estimated from behavioral and health-related protective and risk factors (n = 63,592 adults aged 51–90 years; 45.3% female; mean age, 66.6 ± 9.9 years, from nationally representative surveys). Individual accelerated aging was estimated using gradient-boosting models trained on risk and protective factors (R² = 0.35; r = 0.59; RMSE = 7.55; MAE = 6.09). These indices were linked to country- and period-specific societal values from the World Values Survey (Wave 7), capturing wellbeing, social life, acceptance of rights and choices, and institutional confidence. Higher global societal values were associated with delayed aging (OR = 0.10, 95% CI 0.0973–0.1040), with similar patterns in women and men (both ORs = 0.14, 95% CI 0.13–0.15). Across domains, wellbeing, acceptance of rights and choices, and social life were consistently associated with delayed aging, whereas institutional confidence showed more heterogeneous results. Stronger associations were observed in Latin America (global score OR = 0.01, 95% CI 0.0132–0.0152), compared with more heterogeneous effects in Europe (global score OR = 0.23, 95% CI 0.2115–0.2528), with lower heterogeneity in Western Europe. In contrast, in Eastern Europe, wellbeing and social life remained associated with delayed aging, whereas acceptance of rights and choices and institutional confidence showed no clear associations. Associations persisted after adjustment for socioeconomic status and country-level exposome indicators, including country inequality, air pollution, and political representation. However, social life lost significance after adjustment for gender inequality and political representation. The main effects were validated in a country-stratified hold-out analysis. Overall, findings suggest that more favorable societal values are associated with lower accelerated aging, providing a novel aging-clock model of sociopolitical determinants of health.
